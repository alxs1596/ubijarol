Biometric Passport

Definition

The biometric passport, also known as an electronic passport, is an identity document that, in addition to the use of security paper, contains a polycarbonate sheet with an electronic circuit embedded in it, and uses biometrics to authenticate travelers' citizenship. The incorporation of a tiny RFID chip in the document allows both store additional information and duplicate the one printed on the page containing the passport holder's data, allowing - through public key infrastructure - the certification of the veracity of the data contained in it, making it virtually impossible to forge false identities.

Security Measures

It has a built-in high-tech microchip that stores the information (name, date of birth and signature) and biometric data (fingerprints and face). It has three security measures: microchip (it is a level 3 measure that makes the passport unforgeable), security paper (it has visible holograms, only a special light and nanometric texts can be seen with a microscope) and a complex system (the Passport is issued by a complex integrated software and hardware system that is responsible for the automated execution of each stage of the issuance process, preventing the duplication of a false identity.

Objective

The biometric passport will allow to have free access to the countries of the European Union (EU) that make up the Schengen area, needs to comply with the basic requirement of implementing the biometric passport. The countries that can be entered without a visa are: Germany, Austria, Belgium, Bulgaria, Croatia, Cyprus, Czech Republic, Denmark (without Greenland or the Faroe Islands), Slovakia, Slovenia, Spain, Estonia, Finland, France (without the overseas territories), Greece, Hungary, Italy, Latvia, Liechtenstein, Lithuania, Luxembourg, Malta.
